GETTING STARTED

Introduction to Internet Safety

This page introduces the World Wide Web, explains why children face unique risks today, and frames the skills that help them stay safer.

What is the World Wide Web?

The World Wide Web was developed to make information easily shareable across computers. Proposed by Tim Berners-Lee, it grew rapidly into a global network of websites, services, and connected experiences.

Today, the web powers learning, communication, entertainment, and community—often from the devices that children use every day.

The Rise of Children Online

Access to the internet is increasingly common at younger ages. Alongside this growth comes changing patterns of online behavior, including higher exposure to harmful content and peer-to-peer conflict.

Screen time data also suggests that many children spend more hours online than their adults realize, which increases the need for active guidance and age-appropriate safeguards.

The Digital Generation

Today’s children are often “born connected.” They learn platforms quickly and explore social spaces with little experience of privacy risks, manipulation tactics, and online permanence.

This creates a different challenge than earlier eras: safety is not only about avoiding danger, but also about building judgment and resilience.

Why This Matters

Internet safety affects mental health, education, and social development. Harmful interactions can shape how children feel about themselves, how they concentrate, and how they trust others.

When children understand boundaries and adults use effective controls, the web becomes a place where curiosity can thrive safely.

Safety online is not about fear. It is about informed choices, clear boundaries, and the skills to respond when something feels wrong.
